Direct immunofluorescence (IF) for APP mutant mice brain
Neuropathology

Direct immunofluorescence (IF) for APP mutant mice brain


Project Context:
This project was undertaken to investigate the pathological features of Alzheimer's Disease (AD), specifically focusing on the role of amyloid-beta (Aβ) plaques in neurodegeneration. AD is the most prevalent form of dementia, characterized by cognitive decline and memory loss, impacting millions globally.

Objective:
The primary objective was to utilize molecular biology techniques to quantify and visualize the accumulation of Aβ plaques in brain tissue, comparing pathological differences between genetically modified APP mutant mice and wild-type controls.

Techniques Employed:
1. Immunofluorescence (IF): This technique was used to visualize Aβ plaques directly within brain tissue sections. Using fluorescently labeled antibodies, this method allowed for the precise localization and quantification of Aβ accumulation, which is crucial for understanding the progression of AD.
2. Western Blotting (WB): Employed to quantitatively assess the levels of Aβ protein in brain lysates. This technique involved tissue homogenization, SDS-PAGE for protein separation, and subsequent transfer to membranes for detection using specific antibodies.

Procedure Overview:
1. Immunofluorescence (IF): Tissue sections were washed, blocked, and incubated with primary antibodies against Aβ, followed by fluorescent secondary antibodies. The slides were examined under a confocal microscope to assess plaque distribution and density.
2. Western Blotting (WB): Samples were prepared by homogenizing brain tissues, followed by protein quantification, gel electrophoresis, and transfer to nitrocellulose membranes. Protein bands were visualized using chemiluminescent detection, which highlighted the presence and intensity of Aβ proteins.

Result and Conclusion:
The results from IF demonstrated significant Aβ plaque accumulation in APP mutant mice compared to wild-type, visually confirming the genetic impact on Aβ metabolism. Western blot analysis corroborated these findings by showing higher levels of Aβ proteins in mutant strains. These results support the amyloid hypothesis of Alzheimer's disease, suggesting that genetic factors significantly influence disease pathology and progression.

Cell Assay of Isolated Chicken Hepatocytes incubated with FeSO4 & EDTA


Project Context
The project examines the effects of variable concentrations of a test chemical stressor, with and without a cryoprotectant, on the viability of chicken liver parenchyma cells (hepatocytes) isolated mechanically. This study aseptically maintains the cell suspensions at 4°C over a four-week period to assess the cellular response to these conditions.

Aim and Objective:
The primary objective is to perform and compare cell viability assays of mechanically-isolated suspensions of hepatocytes to study the impact of various concentrations of a chemical stressor both with and without the addition of a cryoprotectant.

Procedure Overview:
1. Cell Viability Assay by Trypan Blue Exclusion: This assay uses trypan blue, a dye that cannot penetrate intact cell membranes, thus staining only the dead cells where the membrane integrity is compromised.
2. Isolation of Liver Parenchyma Cells/Hepatocytes by Mechanical Method: This involves mechanically shredding the tissue and filtering it through sieves, a method preferred over enzymatic disaggregation to avoid complex procedures.
3. Effects of Chemical Stressors and Cryoprotectants: Explores the impact of cytotoxic agents like mercury and the protective effects of cryoprotectants such as glycerol on cell viability during preservation.

Materials and Methods:
1. Preparation of Cleaning Solution: Involves creating a K2Cr2O7 and H2SO4 cleaning solution used for oxidizing organic matter and cleaning glassware.
2. Preparation of Phosphate Buffered Saline (PBS): Detailed steps for preparing PBS, used as a medium and buffer in various procedures.
3. Preparation of Trypan Blue Exclusion Dye: Describes the preparation of the dye used for the viability assays.
4. Isolation of Hepatocytes by Mechanical Method: Outlines the procedure for isolating liver cells through mechanical disaggregation.
5. Establishment and Maintenance of Hepatocyte Suspensions: Describes how to prepare and maintain suspensions of hepatocytes with stressors and cryoprotectants at controlled temperatures.
6. Cell Viability Assay by Trypan Blue Exclusion:: Detailed procedure for assessing the viability of the cell suspensions over a four-week period.

Result and Conclusion:
The study revealed a noticeable decline in cell viability across the testing phases, predominantly influenced by the applied chemical stress and the use of cryoprotectants. Evaluated live and dead cell counts highlighted the hepatocytes' response under these conditions. The variability in results indicates the need for improved experimental control and suggests that enhancements in technique and environmental conditions could lead to more consistent and reliable data. The findings stress the critical nature of maintaining stringent aseptic conditions and the provision of essential nutrients to sustain cell viability in vitro
Micropropagation of Brahmi (Bacopa monnieri) Using Nodal Cultures

Micropropagation of Brahmi (Bacopa monnieri) Using Nodal Cultures


Project Context
This project focused on the micropropagation of Brahmi (Bacopa monnieri), a medicinal plant known for its therapeutic properties. The aim was to enhance propagation techniques using tissue culture methods to ensure rapid and efficient reproduction of the plant, which is crucial for meeting commercial demand and conservation purposes.

Objective:
The objective was to optimize the growth conditions for multiple shoot induction of Brahmi using nodal segments cultured in Murashige and Skoog (MS) media. This involved adjusting the concentrations and combinations of plant growth regulators to maximize shoot proliferation while maintaining strict aseptic conditions.

Techniques Employed:
Tissue Culture: Utilization of in vitro techniques to grow plants under controlled conditions. Sterilization: Application of chemical agents to ensure asepsis during the culturing process. Hormonal Manipulation: Strategic use of cytokinins and auxins to influence growth patterns and shoot induction.

Methods:
1. Culture Preparation: Formulated and prepared MS media, ensuring it contained the necessary macro and micronutrients, vitamins, and optimized concentrations of plant growth regulators. The media was specifically designed to support optimal plant growth and shoot proliferation.
2. Sterilization and Aseptic Techniques: Implemented stringent sterilization protocols using sodium hypochlorite and mercuric chloride to prepare the plant material for inoculation, ensuring all procedures were carried out under laminar airflow to maintain aseptic conditions.
3. Inoculation and Cultivation: Carefully inoculated sterilized Brahmi shoots into the prepared media. The cultures were maintained in controlled environmental conditions, monitoring their growth and development over a four-week period.

Result and Conclusion:
Results: The nodal cultures exhibited significant shoot proliferation, particularly in media formulations with higher concentrations of cytokinins and no auxins. Observations confirmed that the cytokinin-auxin ratio plays a crucial role in determining the morphogenesis of shoots versus roots. Conclusion: The study successfully demonstrated that optimizing the ratio of cytokinins in the growth media could effectively enhance the micropropagation rate of Brahmi. These findings provide a viable method for large-scale production of Brahmi, ensuring a stable supply for pharmaceutical use and further research.

Significance:
The project contributes to the field of plant biotechnology by refining micropropagation techniques that can be applied to other medicinal plants. The ability to rapidly and efficiently propagate Brahmi under controlled conditions not only supports commercial cultivation but also aids in the conservation of this valuable medicinal resource.

Analyzing Lake Health through Chemical Parameter Testing


Project Context:
In this project, I conducted a detailed analysis of the water quality from three significant urban lakes: Byrasandra Lake, Sankey Tank, and Madiwala Lake. The study was aimed at assessing the health of these water bodies over a one-month period by measuring key chemical parameters: dissolved oxygen, alkalinity, and acidity. This project was executed under the guidance of Dr. Mridul Umesh at Christ University's laboratory.

Objectives:
Dissolved Oxygen: To determine the concentration of dissolved oxygen using the modified Winkler’s method, which provides insights into the aerobic environment of the water.
Alkalinity: To measure the water's ability to neutralize acids which helps understand the buffering capacity of the lake against acidification.
Acidity: To assess the free hydrogen ion concentration that indicates potential pollution levels and the presence of acid-generating substances.

Methods and Materials:
1. Sample Collection: Water samples were collected in pre-cleaned plastic bottles, early in the morning to avoid altered readings from atmospheric oxygen interactions.
2. Dissolved Oxygen Analysis: Employed the Winkler's method, involving oxidation-reduction reactions facilitated by specific reagents and titration against sodium thiosulphate.
3. Alkalinity and Acidity Testing: Utilized titration methods involving 0.2 N Sulphuric acid and 0.2 N Sodium Hydroxide to accurately measure the buffering capacity and acid presence in the water samples.

Key Techniques:
Titration: Utilized volumetric analysis to determine chemical concentrations, enhancing precision in quantifying the chemical properties of water.
Quality Control: Ensured reliable results through meticulous sample collection, preservation, and repeated testing for concordant values.
Data Analysis: Interpreted experimental data to evaluate the ecological status of the lakes, using pH metrics and chemical concentration levels.

Result and Conclusion:
The analysis highlighted varied pollution levels across the lakes:

Sankey Tank exhibited relatively higher dissolved oxygen levels but also showed significant alkalinity, which could suggest the presence of bicarbonates and carbonates typical of urban runoff
Madiwala Lake presented lower levels of dissolved oxygen and alkalinity, indicating poor water quality and high microbial pollution
Byrasandra Lake showed moderate levels of both alkalinity and dissolved oxygen, suggesting better water quality compared to Madiwala Lake but still requiring attention.

This project not only provided a comprehensive chemical profile of each lake but also highlighted the urgent need for remedial measures to improve water quality. The findings are crucial for environmental management and policy-making, aiming to restore and preserve the ecological balance of these urban water bodies.
